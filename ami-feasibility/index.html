Market rents in El Paso are, in most cases, already below what a household could afford at 100% of the area median income (AMI). That sounds like good news. It's actually the reason duplexes and ADUs — both already legal almost everywhere in the city — mostly don't get built.

Duplexes are by-right in R-4 and R-5 zoning. ADUs are allowed citywide. The zoning reform for those two housing types is basically done — triplexes are the one type that still needs a zoning change, since they currently require apartment or commercial zoning everywhere. So for duplexes and ADUs, the real question was never why is this illegal. It's why doesn't anyone build it, if it's already legal?

The answer is a construction math problem, not a permitting problem. New-build costs — land, shell, financing, permitting — have to be covered by the rent a new unit can actually charge. And when the market is already clearing $790–850 a month for existing studios and 1-bedrooms nearby, a newly built unit can't charge much more just because it's new. The revenue side of the math is capped by what's already on the market — and in El Paso, that cap sits below what it typically costs to break even on ground-up construction. Regardless of zoning.

A likely objection

Doesn't this mean there's no housing need here?

No — and it's worth being precise about why not, because low rents and unmet housing need aren't actually in tension. They're measuring two different things. Rent level is a price stat. A needs assessment showing significant unmet demand, even below 70% AMI, is an availability stat. A market can have a low average price and still be badly short of decent, available units at that price — especially for the household sizes and unit types the existing stock doesn't match well.

This dataset speaks to price level. It says nothing about vacancy, unit condition, or whether what's on the market actually fits the households who need it. That's where the real shortage lives, and it's a separate question from the one this map answers.

The mechanism

So why doesn't Austin have this problem?

Austin's rent sits further below its own 100% AMI than El Paso's does — Austin's incomes are so much higher that even a healthy market rent looks cheap by comparison. And yet Austin just delivered more than 30,000 new units in a single year, enough to push its own vacancy rate to a decade high and rents down 6% year-over-year. So "rent below AMI" clearly isn't what's stopping construction there.

That's because AMI is a relative number — it scales with each metro's own income level. Construction costs don't scale that way. Materials, labor, and financing vary some by region, but nowhere near as much as incomes do. So "rent vs. AMI" and "rent vs. what it costs to build" are different questions that can point in opposite directions:

Austin's rent is more "below AMI" than El Paso's, but in absolute dollars it comfortably clears construction costs, while El Paso's doesn't. What's actually specific to El Paso isn't rent-below-AMI — that turns out to be common everywhere we checked. It's absolute rent that's low relative to costs that aren't set locally. That's a narrower claim, and the reason this project treats the feasibility map below as the main finding, and the AMI map as useful context.

What actually works here

Two reforms that follow from a feasibility constraint

Since this is a construction-cost problem, not a legalization problem, "expand duplex allowances" doesn't do much — that's already the law. Two levers that follow from where the actual constraint sits:

  • Prioritize adaptive reuse over new construction. Converting an existing structure skips most of the land and shell cost that sinks new-build math, so it can pencil at rents that kill ground-up projects. Our framework treats conversions as able to support more units per structure — up to 8 — in high-vacancy tracts, which isn't a style preference, it's the mechanism that makes low rents compatible with anything getting built at all.
  • Target cost-side reform for new construction, not use-side reform. Raising rents to cover cost isn't available in this market, so the reform that matters is lowering what a project has to cover: parking minimums, permitting timelines, a prescriptive small-multifamily code path, fee waivers, or gap financing for the already-legal quadplex-in-R-4/R-5 case.

Methodology & caveats

How the AMI thresholds are set

  • HUD FY2025 income limits, El Paso, TX MSA, family median income $72,800. Household-size adjustments follow HUD's standard schedule. Affordability = 30% of gross annual income.
  • Two household-size-by-bedroom conventions are shown: the HUD/LIHTC standard (1.5 persons/bedroom) and a simpler 1:1 baseline (bedrooms = persons).

How the feasibility thresholds are derived

  • Inverted from the project's proforma sensitivity model: holding hard costs, land value, hurdle rate, vacancy, and opex at baseline for a 4-unit El Paso infill lot, solved for the minimum rent/sqft needed to clear each feasibility band (Feasible ≥ −3% gap, Feasible w/ subsidy ≥ −10% gap).
  • The high-vacancy cutoff used to unlock more permissive 8-unit thresholds (top quartile, ≥1.4% tract vacancy) is a data-driven default, not a formally adopted project threshold.

What this doesn't capture

  • Every figure reflects active listings (asking rents), not rents paid across the full occupied stock — likely understating affordability at the bottom of the market and modestly overstating it at the top.
  • The Lubbock/Austin comparison uses RentCafe/Zumper estimates, not our own RentCast pipeline — a directional check, not an apples-to-apples benchmark.
  • The time series (May–July 2026, 11 weeks) is a baseline, not yet a settled trend.
